The Brief

Great product.
Deck that didn't show it.

Vaultly had the proof points. 47 paying beta firms, 108% net revenue retention, an NPS of 72 — exceptional numbers for any early-stage SaaS. What they didn't have was a deck that communicated that story with the weight it deserved.

The original materials were generic: stock icons, default fonts, no visual hierarchy, no narrative tension. For investors who see fifty decks a week, generic is invisible.

The Problem

The deck opened with features. It should have opened with chaos — the 8+ hours per employee per week that boutique firms were haemorrhaging chasing documents across four disconnected tools.

Once investors don't feel the pain, they stop caring about the solution.

The Direction

Lead with the problem viscerally. Let the market feel urgent before introducing the product. Sequence traction early — slide 5 of 10 — so by the time investors reach the business model, they already believe the thesis.

Clean, bold, professional. Navy and white with orange as the single accent. A deck that means business.

Design Rationale

Every choice
was deliberate.

01

Problem First. Always.

The original deck opened with features. We moved the problem slide to position two and made it visceral — a stat that hurts, four pain points that make any boutique firm owner nod before you've said a word.

Narrative Structure
02

Navy + Orange. No Apologies.

Not the SaaS default of teal-and-white. Deep navy gives gravitas. Orange as the single accent creates urgency and direction — it tells the eye exactly where to look. Consistent. Disciplined. Memorable.

Colour Strategy
03

Traction Before Business Model

Most decks put traction late. We put it at slide 5. By the time an investor reaches the business model, they already believe the market wants this. The numbers aren't a surprise — they're confirmation.

Slide Sequencing
04

Data That Commands Attention

Six KPIs in a bold grid — no charts, no axes, no legend. When your NRR is 108%, you don't bury it in a line graph. You put it on a pedestal and let it stand alone.

The deck did its job.

Vaultly went into investor conversations with a deck that finally matched the quality of the product they'd built. The traction was real — 47 paying firms, 108% NRR, an NPS of 72. The deck made sure none of that got lost in a wall of bullet points.

The lead investor specifically called out the pitch materials as unusually polished for an early-stage raise. In a room where most seed decks look like they were made in a weekend, that's the difference between a second meeting and a pass.
